Trillium LineFinder

Overview

The Trillium Line Finder is a tool developed by the Oregon Health Plan that is used by medical providers to determine if the combination of a specific diagnosis and procedure is payable by Medicare. I was tasked to redesign the website with a more modernized look and improve search functionality to make navigation of the tool more efficient and intuitive.

Roles

  • User Experience Designer
  • User Experience Researcher

Timeline

May 2023 - August 2023

I. Concept

Problem

The Trillium LineFinder is a tool developed by the Oregon Health Plan that is used by medical providers to search through a prioritized list of services to determine if a line – the combination of an ICD code (representing a diagnosis) and a CPT code (representing a procedure) – is considered ‘above the line’, meaning it is payable by Medicare, or ‘below the line’, meaning it is not.


The tool was originally built in 2011, and as a result it suffered from several security vulnerabilities and sported an outdated design. The goals of the redesign were to meet contemporary security standards; modernize the appearance of the UI; and identify pain points to make navigation of the tool more efficient and intuitive.


II. Research

Exploration of Original Tool

To identify pain points in the original tool, I first sought to explore and familiarize myself with it. Through my personal testing two pain points stood out to me.


1) Challenging Search Interface

There are three major use cases of the tool, corresponding to the three key ways to search: by an ICD and/or CPT code, by a line number, or by a guideline number.


However, in the original tool the fields to enter an ICD code, CPT code, line number and guideline all displayed on the same line, which had given me and other users the false impression that all fields must be filled to conduct a search, which was not the case.


2) Difficulty Viewing Descriptions

In the original tool when searching for a line, its associated ICD and CPT codes displayed in a long vertical table which would often go offscreen and be difficult to skim. Additionally, there was no way to view the descriptions of each code, which was detrimental for users who wanted to further refine their search.


III. Ideation

Design Solutions

Once pain points were identified and validated with users, I moved onto the ideation stage.

1) Reimagining the Search System

To clarify the confusion over the required search criteria created by putting all search fields on the same line, I devised a dropdown with three options corresponding to the four primary ways to search: by ICD and/or CPT code, by line number, by guideline number, and by keyword.


This dropdown offers a cleaner interface and reduces the user’s cognitive load, as search input fields are limited to those required for the selected type of search


For example: if a user selects ‘ICD and/or CPT code’ they will only be shown the ICD code field and CPT code field, but if they were to select ‘line number’ they will only be shown a field to input the line number.


2) Accessible Alternatives to Modal

To address the inability and lack of accessibility to view code descriptions, we designed accessible alternatives.


In the competitor tool, descriptions for codes related to a line are viewable by opening a collapsable table clicking on them. We wished to adopt this design pattern in our redesign, as it allowed users to quickly access and switch between code descriptions.


However, in the competitor, these descriptions displayed as modals - which are pop-up windows that grey out and disable interaction with the main page until they are closed.


This interaction posed accessibility concerns as blind users may not be notified a modal has appeared - and if not properly coded, modals cannot be read by a screen reader or closed by keyboard only.


The devised alternative to modals was to have the descriptions display within the table beneath the list of codes when one of them was clicked.


This addressed the accessibility concerns of using a modal, and kept information easy to access and in-context.


IV. Testing

Usability Testing

After completion and approval of the initial wireframes, I arranged to conduct a usability test with super users.


In preparation of testing I created an interactable prototype for the users to click through to imitate how the final product would look and function. The immersive experience of this simulation was employed to promote more specific data and feedback from users. I also composed multiple use case scenario questions to prompt users to navigate to specific parts of the tool. These questions would give me the opportunity to observe a user’s journey firsthand, and note the participants’ behaviors, goals, and pain points as they interacted with the product.



During the usability test I took detailed notes on the user’s interactions with and comments on the prototype. As I observed the users’ actions throughout each scenario, I saw they were able to quickly navigate to the designated pages, signaling to me that the design was intuitive thus far. Users responded positively to the modernized look and organization of information, remarking that the design was clean, intuitive; and on the collapsable tables and in-table ICD and CPT descriptions, saying it made it much easier to search for things.


One point of constructive criticism made by the users was the request for a disclaimer about whether a specific line is fundable by Medicare.


During an initial meeting with users to understand the functionality of the tool, users had explained that all lines with line numbers below 470 are fundable by Medicare, whereas those above are not fundable. However, during this test users clarified that lines marked as fundable can only be verified as fundable if all associated guidelines of the line are met by the practitioner.


In the past some users had assumed that any line marked as ‘above’ was fundable by Medicare, which led to confusion and medical practice issues. This feedback was indispensable and greatly appreciated. I quickly devised a solution to place a disclaimer explaining that lines are only payable if they are marketed as ‘above’ and meet guideline requirements above the line results table, so it would be visible to users every time they looked at a line.



V. Outcome

Final Prototype

The completed interactable prototype was presented to a group of senior designers, developers, and product managers at Centene.


Senior designers and managers complimented the design and commended the prioritization of Accessibility concerns.


After finalization of the design I transitioned into a design support role to support front-end development.


Development currently ongoing and final website will be deployed sometime in the future.
